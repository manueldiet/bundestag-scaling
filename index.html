Scaling the 20th Bundestag: Ideology, Coalition, and the Geometry of a Vote

Three estimators — a simple SVD, its double-centered cousin, and a Bayesian 2-PL IRT model — applied to 161 roll-call votes of the Ampel coalition. What emerges is less a left–right axis than a strict record of institutional roles: who governed and who opposed.

Introduction

When a parliament votes, it leaves a trace. Each roll call is a small data point about where every legislator stands relative to every other. With enough such traces, one can recover an underlying geometry — a low-dimensional space in which familiar political categories like left, right, or government reappear as coordinates. This is the project of ideal-point estimation, and it has been the workhorse of empirical legislative studies since Poole and Rosenthal's NOMINATE in the 1980s.

This paper applies three such estimators to the namentliche Abstimmungen of the 20th German Bundestag, the Ampel period in which SPD, Bündnis 90/Die Grünen, and the FDP formed a three-party coalition from September 2021 until its collapse in March 2025.

Crucially, we incorporate a legislative codebook that tracks whether each roll-call motion was ultimately accepted or rejected. As we will see, this binary indicator fundamentally changes how we interpret the recovered dimensions.

The substantive question is narrow: in the geometry of actual voting behaviour, did the FDP sit closer to the CDU/CSU opposition than to its own coalition partners? The answer forces us to rethink what these spatial models actually measure.

The Data

The dataset comprises 161 roll-call votes across 772 legislators. We coded Yes as 1, No as 0, and handled missingness via modal imputation based on party affiliation, replacing absences with the dominant choice of the legislator's faction.

ii. Baseline SVD

Singular Value Decomposition writes the imputed vote matrix as X = UΣVᵀ. The first column of U gives each legislator a coordinate on the dominant axis of variation.

dim2 <- svd_fit$u[, 2]

Cross-referencing vote loadings with the legislative codebook is decisive: every strongly negative vote is an accepted government initiative; every positive vote is a rejected opposition motion. Dimension 1 is a passage indicator, not a left–right scale.

c) Is Dimension 2 meaningful?

Dimension 2 explains 10.5% of variance. The CDU/CSU is strikingly isolated at −0.060 while all Ampel parties are positive. This plausibly captures how the two opposition blocs oppose: the CDU/CSU files high volumes of procedurally elaborate Anträge; the AfD takes a confrontational protest style. The dimension appears meaningful — it separates disciplined from disruptive opposition — though committee sponsorship patterns may also contribute.

iii. Double-Centered SVD

To strip out baseline tendencies — some MPs vote yes more often; some bills pass more easily — we double-center the matrix before decomposing it:

# Double-centering (R)
X_dc <- X_hat - rowMeans(X_hat) -
  rep(colMeans(X_hat), each = nrow(X_hat)) +
  mean(X_hat)
max(abs(rowMeans(X_dc)))  # → 6.21e-17 ✓
max(abs(colMeans(X_dc)))  # → 5.69e-17 ✓

Verification passes: both row and column means are machine zero (≈ 10−17).

Variance in Dimension 1 drops from 86.1% to 60.5% — roughly a quarter was pure baseline. The party ordering shifts: the CDU/CSU is now the most extreme party, surpassing the AfD. Once we control for baseline yes-rates, the CDU/CSU's high volume of subsequently rejected motions makes them the structural antipode of the government. The FDP remains firmly inside the coalition cluster.

Finding 2 Double-centering reduces but does not eliminate the institutional signature. The CDU/CSU's extreme position reflects their role as the highest-volume, most disciplined opposition force. The FDP remains in the coalition cluster.

iv. Bayesian 2-PL IRT

SVD produces no uncertainty estimates. For substantive inference we need a proper likelihood and posterior. We fit a two-parameter logistic IRT model via brms / Stan.

Model specification

P(y_ij = 1) = logit⁻¹( α_j · (θ_i − β_j) )

θi = legislator ideal point; βj = vote difficulty (indifference point); αj = discrimination (steepness of the yes/no boundary). The difficulty β absorbs the baseline success of a vote, structurally controlling for the coalition effect that dominated the raw SVD.

Priors

v. Substantive Evaluation & Uncertainty

Claim under evaluation

The FDP's position in the Ampel coalition was ideologically closer to CDU/CSU than to SPD/Grüne.

1. Point estimates

Partyθ̄Distance to FDP
SPD −0.5510.410

The FDP is 0.41 units from the SPD versus 0.74 from the CDU/CSU — nearly twice as far. The claim fails on point estimates alone.

2. The full Bayesian posterior

Rather than relying on point estimates, we draw jointly from the posterior distributions of all three party means and compute — for each MCMC sample — whether the FDP is closer to CDU/CSU or to SPD:

# Posterior probability
posterior_draws |>
  mutate(
    d_cdu = abs(theta_fdp - theta_cdu),
    d_spd = abs(theta_fdp - theta_spd)
  ) |>
  summarise(mean(d_cdu < d_spd))
# Result: 0.000

In zero out of thousands of posterior draws does the FDP appear closer to the CDU/CSU. The CDU/CSU is simply too far away — the result carries no remaining uncertainty.

3. Individual vs. group uncertainty

For individual FDP legislators, 95% credible intervals are wide (Thomas Sattelberger: −2.27 to +0.21). But the ordering of parties is far more precisely estimated, because it aggregates across 28 FDP legislators and averages out individual-level noise. High individual uncertainty does not imply high uncertainty about group orderings — with enough members, party-level estimates are tight. This distinction is a key advantage of the IRT approach over SVD.

4. Why the narrative persists

Coalition discipline vs. latent ideology. Roll-call votes are constrained by the Koalitionsvertrag. The FDP likely would have voted differently on many bills had it been in opposition — we measure constrained behavior, not underlying preferences.

Public communication vs. voting behavior. The FDP's loudest interventions — defending the Schuldenbremse, resisting the Heizungsgesetz — happened in cabinet and at party congresses, not on the floor. Roll-call data is blind to all of this.

Finding 3 The Bayesian posterior rejects the claim with P = 0.0%: in every single posterior draw, the FDP is closer to the SPD and Grüne than to the CDU/CSU. This reflects voting behavior under coalition discipline — not necessarily latent ideology.

Reproducibility

Code for all models, along with the raw roll-call data, is available in the project repository. The Bayesian model was fitted using 4 chains × 2000 iterations, target acceptance rate δ = 0.95. All results are fully reproducible from the raw Bundestag vote records.
