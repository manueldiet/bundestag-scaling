Roll-Call Scaling · Vol. 1 · Issue 1

Scaling the 20th Bundestag: Ideology, Coalition, and the Geometry of a Vote

Three estimators — a simple SVD, its double-centered cousin, and a Bayesian 2-PL IRT model — applied to 161 roll-call votes of the Ampel coalition. The inclusion of a legislative codebook reveals that what emerges is less a left–right axis than a strict record of institutional roles: who governed and who opposed.

By Manuel Dieterle Universität Mannheim · School of Social Sciences Wahlperiode 20 · 29 Sep 2021 — 24 Mar 2025

Introduction

When a parliament votes, it leaves a trace. Each roll call is a small data point about where every legislator stands relative to every other. With enough such traces, one can recover an underlying geometry — a low-dimensional space in which familiar political categories like left, right, or government reappear as coordinates. This is the project of ideal-point estimation, and it has been the workhorse of empirical legislative studies since Poole and Rosenthal's NOMINATE in the 1980s.

This paper applies three such estimators to the namentliche Abstimmungen of the 20th German Bundestag, the Ampel period in which SPD, Bündnis 90/Die Grünen, and the FDP formed a three-party coalition from September 2021 until its collapse in March 2025. The estimators run from the simple to the elaborate: an unadjusted Singular Value Decomposition, a double-centered SVD that removes baseline participation effects, and a Bayesian two-parameter logistic IRT model fitted in Stan.

Crucially, we incorporate a legislative codebook that tracks whether each roll-call motion was ultimately accepted or rejected. As we will see, this binary indicator fundamentally changes how we interpret the recovered dimensions.

The substantive question is narrower. The FDP is often described as the coalition's most reluctant partner. We ask whether, in the geometry of actual voting behaviour, the FDP sat closer to the CDU/CSU opposition than to its own coalition partners.

The data

The dataset comprises 161 roll-call votes across 772 legislators. We coded Yes as 1, No as 0, and handled missingness via modal imputation based on party affiliation, effectively replacing absences with the dominant choice of the legislator's faction. Missingness rates varied, with government factions showing high discipline while smaller opposition groups had higher absence rates.

Double-Centered SVD

Double-centering removes the marginal distributions (baseline yes-rates of legislators; baseline pass-rates of votes) before decomposing the matrix. For each element:

X̃ij = Xij − X̄i· − X̄·j + X̄·· 
# Double-centered SVD (R)
X_dc <- scale(X_hat, center = TRUE, scale = FALSE)
X_dc <- t(scale(t(X_dc), center = TRUE, scale = FALSE))

The DC-SVD Dimension 2 (16.7% of variance) shows a cleaner structure than in the raw SVD. The correlation between raw and DC-SVD Dimension 1 is r = 0.706 — the overall party ordering is preserved, but individual CDU/CSU rankings shift substantially.

Bayesian 2-PL IRT

SVD does not produce uncertainty estimates. For substantive inference, one needs a model that returns posterior distributions. We fit a two-parameter logistic IRT model in Stan via brms.

Model Specification

Each legislator i has an ideal point θi. Each vote j has a difficulty βj and a discrimination αj:

P(yij = 1) = logit−1( αj · (θi − βj) )

Intuitively: β is the ideological threshold at which a legislator becomes indifferent; α measures how sharply the vote separates legislators above vs. below that threshold. The difficulty parameter β naturally absorbs the baseline passage rate — solving the same problem that double-centering addressed for SVD, but within a fully probabilistic model.

The IRT correlates most strongly with the raw SVD (r = 0.911) — both estimate the coalition-vs-opposition axis, and the IRT's per-vote β plays the role of double-centering's baseline correction. The lower IRT–DC-SVD correlation (r = 0.821) reflects different handling of CDU/CSU members, who shift substantially in the DC-SVD but sit at moderate IRT scores.

The IRT model also produces legislator-level credible intervals. The FDP has the narrowest mean CI width (0.942) of any party, reflecting exceptional internal voting coherence. The AfD has the widest CIs (1.84), consistent with more heterogeneous behavior.

At the level of point estimates, the FDP is roughly twice as far from the CDU/CSU (0.745) as from the SPD (0.410). In the DC-SVD the gap is even more extreme: FDP to SPD is 0.004 units; FDP to CDU/CSU is 0.075 — nearly twenty times larger. But point estimates are not enough.

2. Posterior probability: using the full distribution

For every posterior draw, we compute simultaneously the distances between the FDP mean, the SPD mean, and the CDU/CSU mean:

# Posterior probability: FDP closer to CDU/CSU than to SPD?
d_to_cdu <- abs(theta_fdp_draws - theta_cdu_draws)
d_to_spd <- abs(theta_fdp_draws - theta_spd_draws)

p_closer_to_cdu_than_spd   <- mean(d_to_cdu < d_to_spd)   # 0.000
p_closer_to_cdu_than_gruen <- mean(d_to_cdu < d_to_gruen) # 0.000
p_closer_to_cdu_than_ampel <- mean(d_to_cdu < d_to_ampel) # 0.000

The posterior probability is 0.0% across all comparisons. Not a single MCMC draw out of thousands supports the claim. The claim is rejected not just on average, but uniformly across the entire posterior distribution.

3. Distinguishing sources of uncertainty

Individual MP uncertainty is high. Thomas Sattelberger (FDP) has a 95% CI of [−2.27, +0.21] — width of nearly 2.5 units. A single legislator's 161-vote record does not tightly identify an ideal point. Notably, the most CDU-leaning FDP member by point estimate — Fabian Griewel at θ̄ = +0.067 — is actually closer to the CDU/CSU mean (distance 0.537) than to the SPD mean (0.618). A minority of FDP members individually sit nearer the CDU/CSU.

Party ordering uncertainty is near zero. Averaging over 28 FDP and 205 CDU/CSU legislators drastically shrinks uncertainty. The FDP party mean is robustly at −0.141 across all posterior draws; CDU/CSU robustly at +0.604. The ordering is not a close call. The Bayesian model handles this correctly: computing party means inside each posterior draw properly propagates individual uncertainty while exploiting pooling across many legislators.

This distinction matters for the political narrative. One can defensibly say “some FDP members vote closer to CDU territory” — a valid individual-level observation. But this does not license the party-level claim that the FDP as a party was closer to CDU than to SPD. The two operate at different levels of the posterior.

Finding 3 The narrative that the FDP was ideologically closer to CDU/CSU than to its coalition partners is rejected at every level of analysis — point estimates, party means, and Bayesian posterior probability (P = 0.0%). But the data does not prove the FDP is “left-wing.” It proves that on the record of publicly cast votes, coalition discipline overrode any ideological proximity to the opposition. The IRT's posterior credible intervals make explicit what SVD obscures: individual-level uncertainty is high, but party-level ordering is essentially certain.

4. Why the narrative persists anyway

Roll-call votes are a partial window, heavily confounded by Fraktionsdisziplin. They miss internal negotiations, public communications, and the cabinet-level disputes — the Heizungsgesetz, the debt brake standoff, electoral reform — in which the FDP's distance from its partners was most politically visible. Because the primary dimension maps nearly perfectly onto whether a bill was an accepted government initiative or a rejected opposition motion, the geometry we recover is the geometry of publicly cast votes. To measure true ideological distance independently of institutional constraints, future work must look beyond roll-calls — to text analysis of speeches, committee voting, or amendment proposals, where Fraktionsdisziplin is less absolute.

Code for all models, along with the raw roll-call data, is available in the project repository.
