Roll-Call Scaling · Vol. 1 · Issue 1

Scaling the 20th Bundestag: Ideology, Coalition, and the Geometry of a Vote

Three estimators — a simple SVD, its double-centered cousin, and a Bayesian 2-PL IRT model — applied to 161 roll-call votes of the Ampel coalition. The inclusion of a legislative codebook reveals that what emerges is less a left–right axis than a strict record of institutional roles: who governed and who opposed.

By Manuel Dieterle Universität Mannheim · School of Social Sciences Wahlperiode 20 · 29 Sep 2021 — 24 Mar 2025

Introduction

When a parliament votes, it leaves a trace. Each roll call is a small data point about where every legislator stands relative to every other. With enough such traces, one can recover an underlying geometry — a low-dimensional space in which familiar political categories like left, right, or government reappear as coordinates. This is the project of ideal-point estimation, and it has been the workhorse of empirical legislative studies since Poole and Rosenthal's NOMINATE in the 1980s.

This paper applies three such estimators to the namentliche Abstimmungen — the recorded, name-by-name votes — of the 20th German Bundestag, the so-called Ampel period in which SPD, Bündnis 90/Die Grünen, and the FDP formed a three-party coalition from September 2021 until its collapse in March 2025. The estimators run from the simple to the elaborate: an unadjusted Singular Value Decomposition, a double-centered SVD that removes baseline participation effects, and a Bayesian two-parameter logistic IRT model fitted in Stan.

Crucially, we incorporate a legislative codebook that tracks whether each roll-call motion was ultimately accepted or rejected. As we will see, this binary indicator fundamentally changes how we interpret the recovered dimensions.

The substantive question is narrower. The FDP is often described as the coalition's most reluctant partner. We ask whether, in the geometry of actual voting behaviour, the FDP sat closer to the CDU/CSU opposition than to its own coalition partners. The answer, perhaps surprisingly, forces us to rethink what these spatial models actually measure.

The data

The dataset comprises 161 roll-call votes across 772 legislators. We coded Yes as 1, No as 0, and handled missingness via modal imputation based on party affiliation, effectively replacing absences with the dominant choice of the legislator's faction. Missingness rates varied, with government factions showing high discipline while smaller opposition groups had higher absence rates.

Baseline SVD

Singular Value Decomposition is the simplest estimator that can credibly be called ideal-point. It writes the imputed matrix as the product X = UΣVᵀ, and the first column of U — scaled by the leading singular value — gives each legislator a position on the dominant dimension of variation.

# Baseline SVD (R)
svd_fit <- svd(X_hat)
legislator_coords <- svd_fit$u %*% diag(svd_fit$d)
dim1 <- legislator_coords[, 1]
dim2 <- legislator_coords[, 2]

The first dimension absorbs 86.1% of the total variance — an enormous share that immediately suggests one structural cleavage dominates everything else. The second dimension picks up a further 10.5%, and a third drops to a thin 2.1%.

What does dimension one represent?

Inspecting the party means tells the story. All Ampel parties cluster tightly around −0.042; the opposition parties (CDU/CSU, AfD, Linke) sit at less-negative values. However, checking the most heavily loaded votes against our legislative codebook explains why. The votes with the most extreme negative loadings (e.g., "Suizidprävention stärken") were exclusively successful government motions (accepted = True). Conversely, votes with positive loadings were exclusively failed opposition motions (accepted = False). The dimension is, in effect, a coalition axis: it separates those who passed laws from those who filed rejected alternatives.

Double-centered SVD

If Dimension 1 is dominated by a coalition effect, what remains once that effect is removed? The standard answer is to double-centre the matrix — subtract row, column, and grand means — before decomposing it. This is widely expected to strip out baseline tendencies (some MPs vote yes more often; some votes pass more easily) and leave only the interaction structure, often interpreted as true ideology.

# Double-centered SVD (R)
X_dc <- scale(X_hat, center = TRUE, scale = FALSE)
X_dc <- t(scale(t(X_dc), center = TRUE, scale = FALSE))
dc_fit <- svd(X_dc)

The variance share of Dimension 1 collapses from 86.1% to 60.5%. The missing roughly 25 percentage points were pure baseline. However, cross-referencing the new DC-SVD vote loadings with the codebook reveals a striking reality: the most negative votes are still exclusively accepted government bills, and the most positive are still rejected opposition bills. Double-centering did not remove the government-vs-opposition effect; it merely centered it around zero. The picture rearranges, but the logic remains institutional:

The reordering is informative. The CDU/CSU emerges as the most extreme party once baselines are removed. This does not mean they are ideologically further right than the AfD. The reading is institutional: the CDU/CSU introduced the most motions that were subsequently rejected by the Ampel, and consistently voted against Ampel bills. They are the structural antipode to the government.

Finding 2 Even after removing baseline effects, the primary dimension is heavily tied to institutional roles rather than pure ideology. The CDU/CSU's extreme position reflects their high volume of rejected motions and disciplined opposition.

Bayesian 2-PL IRT

SVD is fast and well-understood, but it does not produce uncertainty estimates. For substantive inference — for any sentence that begins "the FDP is probably …" — one needs a model that returns posterior distributions, not point estimates. We fit a two-parameter logistic Item-Response Theory model in Stan, via brms.

Model

Each legislator i has an ideal point θi. Each vote j has a difficulty βj and a discrimination αj. The probability that legislator i votes yes on vote j is:

P(y_ij = 1) = logit⁻¹( α_j · (θ_i − β_j) )

Intuitively: each vote is a question with some difficulty (β: where on the ideological dimension is the indifference point?) and some discrimination (α: how cleanly does the vote separate the chamber?). The difficulty parameter β naturally controls for the baseline success of a vote — effectively absorbing whether a motion was a guaranteed government victory (accepted = True) or a hopeless opposition statement (accepted = False).

Substantive findings

The motivating question was narrow: was the FDP, ideologically, closer to the CDU/CSU than to its coalition partners? Three pieces of evidence inform the answer.

1. The party means

On every estimator, the FDP's mean sits inside the coalition cluster, not the opposition one. In the DC-SVD, the FDP is at −0.026 while SPD and Grüne are at −0.030; the CDU/CSU is at +0.049. The straight-line distance from the FDP to the SPD is roughly 0.004; to the CDU/CSU, 0.075 — an order of magnitude larger.

2. The posterior probability

Point estimates are not enough; we want the posterior probability that the claim holds. Using the IRT posterior draws:

# Posterior probability that FDP is closer to CDU than SPD
posterior <- as_draws_df(fit_2pl)

theta_fdp <- posterior$theta_FDP
theta_spd <- posterior$theta_SPD
theta_cdu <- posterior$theta_CDU

d_to_cdu <- abs(theta_fdp - theta_cdu)
d_to_spd <- abs(theta_fdp - theta_spd)

p_claim <- mean(d_to_cdu < d_to_spd)
# Preliminary result: P ≈ 0.11

The posterior probability that the FDP sat closer to the CDU/CSU than to the SPD is approximately 0.11 — that is, the data place roughly nine-to-one odds against the popular framing. However, knowing that our underlying dimension heavily measures institutional conformity changes how we view this probability.

Finding 3 The narrative of an "FDP closer to CDU than to SPD" is not supported by roll-call evidence. But crucially, the data does not prove the FDP is "left-wing"; it simply proves that the FDP voted with flawless government discipline, overriding any underlying ideological proximity to the opposition.

3. Why the narrative persists anyway

Roll-call votes are a partial window, heavily confounded by Fraktionsdisziplin (party discipline). They miss internal negotiations, public communications, and the cabinet-level disputes (Heizungsgesetz, debt brake, electoral reform) in which the FDP's distance from its partners was most visible.

Because the primary dimension maps perfectly onto whether a bill was an accepted government initiative or a rejected opposition motion, the geometry we recover here is the geometry of publicly cast votes. In that arena, coalition agreements overrode interpersonal or ideological proximity. To measure true ideological distance, future work must look beyond roll-calls to text analysis of speeches or committee records, where the constraints of government discipline are less absolute.

Reproducibility

Code for all models, along with the raw roll-call data, is available in the project repository. The Bayesian model was fitted using 4 chains of 2000 iterations each, with target acceptance rates adjusted to 0.95 to eliminate divergent transitions.
