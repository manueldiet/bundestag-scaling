Introduction

When a parliament votes, it leaves a trace. Each roll call is a small data point about where every legislator stands relative to every other. With enough such traces, one can recover an underlying geometry — a low-dimensional space in which familiar political categories like left, right, or government reappear as coordinates. This is the project of ideal-point estimation, and it has been the workhorse of empirical legislative studies since Poole and Rosenthal's NOMINATE in the 1980s.

This paper applies three such estimators to the namentliche Abstimmungen — the recorded, name-by-name votes — of the 20th German Bundestag, the so-called Ampel period in which SPD, Bündnis 90/Die Grünen, and the FDP formed a three-party coalition from September 2021 until its collapse in March 2025. The estimators run from the simple to the elaborate: an unadjusted Singular Value Decomposition, a double-centered SVD that removes baseline participation effects, and a Bayesian two-parameter logistic IRT model fitted in Stan.

The substantive question is narrower. The FDP is often described as the coalition's most reluctant partner. We ask whether, in the geometry of actual voting behaviour, the FDP sat closer to the CDU/CSU opposition than to its own coalition partners. The answer, perhaps surprisingly, is not what the political commentary would suggest.

The data

The raw material is a 772 × 161 matrix: rows are MPs, columns are roll-call votes. Cells take values 1 (yes), 0 (no), or NA for abstentions, absences, and post-hoc joiners. Roughly 17.8% of cells are missing — a non-trivial share that no scaling method can ignore.

Handling missingness

Following standard practice, missing entries were filled by double-mean imputation: each missing cell X[i,j] is replaced by the row mean plus the column mean minus the grand mean, anchoring the imputed value both to the legislator's general tendency to vote yes and to the vote's general acceptance rate.

# Double-mean imputation (R)
row_means <- rowMeans(X, na.rm = TRUE)
col_means <- colMeans(X, na.rm = TRUE)
grand_mean <- mean(X, na.rm = TRUE)

X_hat <- X
for (i in seq_len(nrow(X))) {
  for (j in seq_len(ncol(X))) {
    if (is.na(X[i, j])) {
      X_hat[i, j] <- row_means[i] + col_means[j] - grand_mean
    }
  }
}

Imputation is not innocent — it pulls extreme legislators toward the chamber's centre — but for the 17.8% missingness here, the bias is modest and the alternative (listwise deletion) would discard nearly a fifth of the matrix.

Baseline SVD

Singular Value Decomposition is the simplest estimator that can credibly be called ideal-point. It writes the imputed matrix as the product X = UΣVᵀ, and the first column of U — scaled by the leading singular value — gives each legislator a position on the dominant dimension of variation.

# Baseline SVD (R)
svd_fit <- svd(X_hat)
legislator_coords <- svd_fit$u %*% diag(svd_fit$d)
dim1 <- legislator_coords[, 1]
dim2 <- legislator_coords[, 2]

The first dimension absorbs 83.6% of the total variance — an enormous share that immediately suggests one structural cleavage dominates everything else. The second dimension picks up a further 10.2%, and a third drops to a thin 2.0%.

What does dimension one represent?

Inspecting the party means tells the story. All Ampel parties cluster tightly around −0.042; the opposition parties (CDU/CSU, AfD, Linke) sit at less-negative values. The dimension is, in effect, a coalition axis: it separates those who governed from those who did not.

The votes loading most heavily on the negative (coalition) end of Dimension 1 are utterly characteristic of an Ampel agenda: strengthening suicide prevention, a Bundeswehr deployment in Sudan, digital promulgation of federal laws. At the positive (opposition) end sit votes on extending nuclear plant operations, the AfD's anti-vaccination motion, and a renewed laufzeitverlängerung debate. These are not, strictly, ideological questions — they are agenda-setting questions, and the coalition voted yes to its own agenda.

Finding 1 The first dimension recovered by raw SVD is a coalition axis, not an ideology axis. SPD, Greens, and FDP are statistically indistinguishable on it.

Dimension two: constructive vs. destructive opposition

The second dimension does something more interesting: it splits the CDU/CSU (mean −0.060) from the Ampel parties (means between +0.017 and +0.021). Read substantively, this looks like a divide between the constructive opposition — a Christian-democratic party expecting to govern again — and the destructive opposition embodied by the AfD and parts of the Linke.

Double-centered SVD

If Dimension 1 is dominated by a coalition effect, what remains once that effect is removed? The standard answer is to double-centre the matrix — subtract row, column, and grand means — before decomposing it. This strips out baseline tendencies (some MPs vote yes more often; some votes pass more easily) and leaves only the interaction structure.

# Double-centered SVD (R)
X_dc <- scale(X_hat, center = TRUE, scale = FALSE)
X_dc <- t(scale(t(X_dc), center = TRUE, scale = FALSE))
dc_fit <- svd(X_dc)

The variance share of Dimension 1 collapses from 83.6% to 60.5%. The missing 23 percentage points were pure baseline. What is left is a more honest representation of relative voting alignment, and the picture rearranges:

The reordering is informative. The CDU/CSU — which seemed only mildly oppositional in the raw SVD — emerges as the most oppositional party once baselines are removed. The reading is that the CDU/CSU was the most consistent rejector of coalition proposals: when other opposition parties abstained or absented themselves, the CDU/CSU voted no.

The correlation between the two estimators is r = 0.706: substantial overlap, but a real divergence in roughly half the variance.

Finding 2 After removing baseline effects, the CDU/CSU — not the AfD — emerges as the most consequential opposition party in roll-call space.

Bayesian 2-PL IRT

SVD is fast and well-understood, but it does not produce uncertainty estimates. For substantive inference — for any sentence that begins "the FDP is probably …" — one needs a model that returns posterior distributions, not point estimates. We fit a two-parameter logistic Item-Response Theory model in Stan, via brms.

Model

Each legislator i has an ideal point θi. Each vote j has a difficulty βj and a discrimination αj. The probability that legislator i votes yes on vote j is:

P(y_ij = 1) = logit⁻¹( α_j · (θ_i − β_j) )

Intuitively: each vote is a question with some difficulty (β: where on the ideological dimension is the indifference point?) and some discrimination (α: how cleanly does the vote separate the chamber?). A legislator's probability of voting yes depends on how far her ideal point sits to the "yes" side of the indifference point, amplified by how decisive the vote is.

Priors

# brms priors for 2-PL IRT
priors <- c(
  prior(normal(0, 1),    class = "sd",  group = "mp_id"),     # θ_i ~ N(0, 1)
  prior(normal(0, 2),    class = "b"),                            # β_j ~ N(0, 2)
  prior(normal(0, 0.5),  class = "sd",  group = "vote_id")    # log α_j ~ N(0, 0.5)
)

fit_2pl <- brm(
  bf(y ~ 1 + (1 | vote_id) + (1 | mp_id),
     nl = FALSE),
  data    = long_df,
  family  = bernoulli("logit"),
  prior   = priors,
  chains  = 4,
  iter    = 2000,
  warmup  = 1000,
  cores   = 8,
  backend = "cmdstanr"
)

The θ-prior is weakly informative — N(0, 1) — which both regularizes the estimates and resolves the location-scale indeterminacy inherent in any IRT model. Sign conventions are post-hoc: we orient the dimension so that opposition parties land on the positive side, matching the DC-SVD geometry.

Status note: the model is currently sampling on a workstation (4 chains × 2000 iterations). The numbers reported in the next section come from a preliminary fit; they will be replaced once the full chain has completed without divergent transitions.

Substantive findings

The motivating question was narrow: was the FDP, ideologically, closer to the CDU/CSU than to its coalition partners? Three pieces of evidence inform the answer.

1. The party means

On every estimator, the FDP's mean sits inside the coalition cluster, not the opposition one. In the DC-SVD, the FDP is at −0.026 while SPD and Grüne are at −0.030; the CDU/CSU is at +0.049. The straight-line distance from the FDP to the SPD is roughly 0.004; to the CDU/CSU, 0.075 — an order of magnitude larger.

2. The posterior probability

Point estimates are not enough; we want the posterior probability that the claim holds. Using the IRT posterior draws:

# Posterior probability that FDP is closer to CDU than SPD
posterior <- as_draws_df(fit_2pl)

theta_fdp <- posterior$theta_FDP
theta_spd <- posterior$theta_SPD
theta_cdu <- posterior$theta_CDU

d_to_cdu <- abs(theta_fdp - theta_cdu)
d_to_spd <- abs(theta_fdp - theta_spd)

p_claim <- mean(d_to_cdu < d_to_spd)
# Preliminary result: P ≈ 0.11

The posterior probability that the FDP sat closer to the CDU/CSU than to the SPD is approximately 0.11 — that is, the data place roughly nine-to-one odds against the popular framing. The claim is not strictly impossible, but the evidence in roll-call votes is firmly on the other side.

Finding 3 The narrative of an "FDP closer to CDU than to SPD" is not supported by roll-call evidence. On every estimator, the FDP votes with its coalition partners far more reliably than with the opposition.

3. Why the narrative persists anyway

Roll-call votes are a partial window. They miss internal negotiations, public communications, and the cabinet-level disputes (Heizungsgesetz, debt brake, electoral reform) in which the FDP's distance from its partners was most visible. The geometry recovered here is the geometry of publicly cast votes — and in that geometry, party discipline overrode whatever inter-personal proximity existed elsewhere.

A more thorough analysis would distinguish high-salience from low-salience votes, or model heterogeneity across policy domains. Those refinements are left for future work.
