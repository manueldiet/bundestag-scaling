```
# Comprehensive Guide to Legislative Scaling and Ideal Point Estimation

## 1. Introduction to Scaling
* **Core Goal:** To place political actors (legislators, parties) and/or choice items (roll-call votes, bills, survey questions) onto a common, low-dimensional latent scale (typically 1-dimensional or 2-dimensional, such as a Left-Right ideological spectrum).
* **Data Inputs:** Primarily binary roll-call voting matrices ($Y_{ij} \in \{0, 1\}$), where rows represent legislators and columns represent votes. It can also be applied to survey responses or text co-occurrence matrices.
* **Methodological Approaches:**
  1. **Matrix Factorization Methods:** Deterministic geometric reductions (e.g., Multidimensional Scaling [MDS], Singular Value Decomposition [SVD], Principal Component Analysis [PCA]).
  2. **Parametric Models:** Probabilistic frameworks based on latent traits (e.g., Item Response Theory [IRT]).
  3. **Non-parametric Models:** Classification techniques optimized for spatial cutting lines (e.g., Optimal Classification).

---

## 2. Missing Data & Imputation (Pre-processing)
* **The Problem:** Non-parametric matrix methods (like SVD) require a complete, rectangular matrix. Real-world roll-call datasets are inherently incomplete because Members of Parliament (MPs) miss votes due to illness, travel, or strategic abstentions ($NA$).
* **Double-Mean Imputation Formula:**
  $$\hat{X}_{ij} = \bar{X}_{i\cdot} + \bar{X}_{\cdot j} - \bar{X}_{\cdot\cdot}$$
  * $\bar{X}_{i\cdot}$: The row mean (the average voting tendency of legislator $i$ across all their observed votes).
  * $\bar{X}_{\cdot j}$: The column mean (the average support/popularity level of bill $j$ across all observed legislators).
  * $\bar{X}_{\cdot\cdot}$: The grand mean (the overall baseline rate of "yes" votes in the entire dataset).
* **Intuition:** Instead of just filling missing values with a flat global average, double-mean imputation accounts for two facts simultaneously: how agreeable a specific legislator is in general, and how popular that specific bill was across the whole parliament. The grand mean is subtracted to avoid double-counting the baseline dataset density.

---

## 3. Matrix Methods: Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)
* **Mathematical Definition:** Any real rectangular $n \times m$ matrix $X$ can be factored into three matrices:
  $$X = U D V^T$$
  * $U$ ($n \times n$ orthogonal matrix): Contains the left-singular vectors. These represent the latent coordinates or positions of the rows (**legislators**).
  * $D$ ($n \times m$ diagonal matrix): Contains non-negative singular values $d_j$ sorted in descending order ($d_1 \ge d_2 \ge \dots \ge d_m$). These values scale the importance of each dimension.
  * $V$ ($m \times m$ orthogonal matrix): Contains the right-singular vectors. These represent the latent coordinates or positions of the columns (**votes/bills**).
* **Low-Rank Approximation (Truncated SVD):** To reduce dimensionality and filter out noise, we retain only the top $k$ dimensions:
  $$X \approx U_k D_k V_k^T$$
* **Variance Explained:** The proportion of total variance captured by the very first latent dimension is calculated using the singular values from the $D$ component:
  $$var_1 = \frac{d_1^2}{\sum_j d_j^2}$$

---

## 4. Advanced Geometric Pre-processing: Double-Centering
* **Motivation:** Raw voting matrices contain strong main effects. For instance, some bills pass almost unanimously, and some legislators vote "yes" to almost everything. If you run SVD on raw data, the first dimension will simply capture these baseline popularities rather than actual ideological differences. Double-centering extracts these main effects before decomposition.
* **Double-Centering Formula:**
  $$\tilde{X}_{ij} = X_{ij} - \bar{X}_{i\cdot} - \bar{X}_{\cdot j} + \bar{X}_{\cdot\cdot}$$
* **Mathematical Properties:** The resulting double-centered matrix $\tilde{X}$ is structurally shifted so that the sum (and mean) of every single row and column equals exactly zero:
  $$\sum_j \tilde{X}_{ij} = 0 \quad \forall i \quad \text{and} \quad \sum_i \tilde{X}_{ij} = 0 \quad \forall j$$
* **Intuition:** By stripping away how frequently an MP votes "yes" and how frequently a bill gets "yes" votes, SVD is forced to run strictly on the *interaction* between MPs and bills. This shifts the focus from "who shows up and agrees with the mainstream" to "who deviates from the mainstream in a systematic, polarized way".

---

## 5. Parametric Models: Item Response Theory (IRT)
* **Concept:** Instead of treating votes as deterministic geometric points, IRT models voting behavior as a probabilistic outcome driven by an underlying latent trait (the ideal point).
* **The 1-Dimensional 2-PL (Two-Parameter Logistic) Model:**
  $$P(Y_{ij} = 1) = \text{logit}^{-1}(\beta_j \theta_i - \alpha_j) = \frac{\exp(\beta_j \theta_i - \alpha_j)}{1 + \exp(\beta_j \theta_i - \alpha_j)}$$
* **Parameter Breakdown:**
  * $\theta_i$ (**Ideal Point / Latent Trait**): The ideological position of legislator $i$ on the latent scale (e.g., how economically conservative or progressive they are).
  * $\beta_j$ (**Discrimination Parameter**): Measures how strongly a vote is driven by ideology. If $\beta_j$ is high, the bill cleanly splits legislators along ideological lines. If $\beta_j \approx 0$, the vote is non-ideological or chaotic.
  * $\alpha_j$ (**Difficulty / Cutpoint Parameter**): The baseline hurdle or structural propensity to vote "yes" on bill $j$, regardless of ideology.
* **Political Science Equivalence:** This parametric formulation maps perfectly onto the classic spatial voting model (Clinton, Jackman, and Rivers 2004), where political actors maximize utility over policy alternatives under logistic error terms.

---

## 6. Bayesian Estimation, Priors, and Identification
* **Estimation via MCMC:** Because IRT models feature thousands of parameters simultaneously ($\theta$, $\beta$, and $\alpha$), they are typically estimated using Bayesian Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithms via packages like `brms` (R) or `Stan`/`PyMC` (Python).
* **Standard Informative Priors:**
  * $\theta_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$: Anchors the location (mean at 0) and scale (variance of 1) of the latent dimension.
  * $\beta_j, \alpha_j \sim \text{Weakly informative normal priors}$ (to stabilize estimation).
* **The Identification Problem (Invariance):** The likelihood function of an IRT model cannot distinguish between a specific scale and its mirror image. Multiplying all $\theta_i$ and $\beta_j$ by $-1$ results in the exact same probabilities. 
* **Resolution:** To prevent the scale from flipping arbitrarily during MCMC chains, you must apply post-processing sign constraints—for example, forcing a known left-wing legislator to always have a negative $\theta$ value, which anchors the direction of the entire spectrum.

---

## 7. Statistical Inference and Uncertainty Analysis
* **The SVD vs. IRT Dichotomy:** SVD provides a single, rigid point estimate for each MP, but offers no measure of statistical confidence or error. Bayesian IRT yields a full posterior distribution for every parameter.
* **Two Distinct Levels of Uncertainty:**
  1. **Individual Position Uncertainty:** Evaluated via the posterior variance or Credible Intervals (e.g., 95% CI) of a single legislator's $\theta_i$. This indicates how precisely we can pin down that specific individual's ideology based on their voting history.
  2. **Ordinal / Pairwise Uncertainty:** Evaluates whether we can confidently state that Legislator $A$ is to the right of Legislator $B$. Even if their individual 95% CIs overlap slightly, their relative positions might be highly stable across MCMC iterations.
* **Calculating Pairwise Ordering Probability:** The true confidence that legislator $A$ is more conservative than legislator $B$ is extracted by looking across all $S$ samples of the MCMC chains:
  $$P(\theta_A > \theta_B \mid Y) = \frac{1}{S}\sum_{s=1}^S \mathbb{I}(\theta_{A}^{(s)} > \theta_{B}^{(s)})$$
  where $\mathbb{I}$ is an indicator function that equals 1 if the condition is met in sample $s$, and 0 otherwise.
```

```
## 8. Multidimensional Scaling (MDS) & Proximity Models
* **Core Concept:** Unlike SVD, which directly factorizes the rectangular legislator-by-vote matrix, Multidimensional Scaling (MDS) operates on a square $n \times n$ **dissimilarity (or distance) matrix** $D$, where each entry $d_{ij}$ represents how "differently" legislator $i$ and legislator $j$ voted across all bills.
* **Classical (Metric) MDS:** Tries to find low-dimensional coordinates $x_i, x_j \in \mathbb{R}^k$ such that the Euclidean distances between them match the observed dissimilarities as closely as possible:
  $$\min_{X} \sum_{i<j} \left( \|x_i - x_j\| - d_{ij} \right)^2$$
* **Non-Metric MDS:** Used when voting differences are treated as ordinal rather than interval data. It fits a monotonic transformation $f(d_{ij})$ to preserve only the *rank order* of distances, minimizing an objective function called **Stress**:
  $$\text{Stress} = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{i<j} \left( f(d_{ij}) - \|x_i - x_j\| \right)^2}{\sum_{i<j} \|x_i - x_j\|^2}}$$
* **Intuition:** Imagine you only know the driving distances between a list of cities, but not their geographical coordinates. MDS acts like a mapmaker that uses these distances to reconstruct a 2D map of the cities. In political scaling, "cities" are legislators, and "distance" is how often they disagree.

---

## 9. Dynamic Ideal Point Estimation (Scaling Over Time)
* **The Problem:** In longitudinal datasets (across multiple years or legislative periods like *Wahlperioden*), legislators change their strategic behavior, switch parties, or drift ideologically. Standard static SVD or IRT models assume an MP's position $\theta_i$ is perfectly constant, which masks temporal polarization.
* **The State-Space / Random Walk Framework:** To allow positions to evolve smoothly over time periods $t$, we specify a dynamic prior for the ideal points:
  $$\theta_{i,t} \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\theta_{i,t-1}, \sigma_\omega^2\right)$$
  where $\sigma_\omega^2$ is the evolution variance (evolution parameter) controlling how much an MP's position can change from one period to the next.
* **Intuition:** This model balances two opposing forces: the data from period $t$ pulling the MP toward their current voting behavior, and the random walk prior anchoring them to their past self ($\theta_{i,t-1}$). This prevents erratic, noisy jumps from vote to vote while capturing long-term trends like a party shifting further to the extremes.

---

## 10. Item Characteristic Curves (ICC) & Ideological Cutpoints
* **Visualizing the 2-PL IRT Model:** The probability of an MP voting "yes" ($Y_{ij}=1$) is mapped as an S-shaped logistic curve (the Item Characteristic Curve) along the ideological axis $\theta$.
* **The Inflection Point (Cutpoint):** The exact location on the ideological spectrum where an MP has an exact 50/50 chance of voting "yes" or "no" is defined by the ratio of the item parameters:
  $$\theta_{\text{cut}} = \frac{\alpha_j}{\beta_j}$$
* **Visual Interpretation of Parameters:**
  * **Discrimination ($\beta_j$) as Slope:** The steeper the curve at the cutpoint, the more heavily polarized the vote is. A perfectly vertical line means everyone to the left votes "no" and everyone to the right votes "yes" (perfect ideological sorting).
  * **Difficulty ($\alpha_j$) as Shift:** If a bill is highly popular (high baseline agreement), the entire S-curve shifts horizontally, meaning even highly critical or opposition MPs have a high baseline probability of voting "yes".

---

## 11. Model Evaluation, Fit Diagnostics & Baselines
* **The Problem:** How do we know if a 1D or 2D latent space actually explains the parliament, or if MPs are just voting randomly? We need to compare our scaling results against a naive baseline.
* **The Classification Baseline:** The simplest baseline is the **Majority Roll-Call Guess**. If a bill passed with 70% "yes" votes, a naive model simply guesses "yes" for *every* legislator, yielding a baseline accuracy of 70%.
* **Aggregate Proportion of Reduction in Error (APRE):** Measures how many of the baseline classification errors are completely eliminated by introducing our latent ideal points:
  $$\text{APRE} = \frac{\sum_j \text{Errors}(\text{Majority}_j) - \sum_j \text{Errors}(\text{Model}_j)}{\sum_j \text{Errors}(\text{Majority}_j)}$$
  * An $\text{APRE} = 0$ means the spatial model performs no better than just guessing the majority outcome.
  * An $\text{APRE} = 1$ means the spatial model predicts every single individual vote perfectly.
* **Geometric Mean Probability (GMP):** Evaluates the probabilistic fit of the IRT model by taking the exponent of the average log-likelihood:
  $$\text{GMP} = \exp\left( \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i} \sum_{j} \ln P(Y_{ij} = y_{ij} \mid \theta_i, \beta_j, \alpha_j) \right)$$
  where $N$ is the total number of non-missing votes. GMP ranges from 0.5 (random guessing on binary choices) to 1.0 (absolute certainty).
```

```
* **Core Concept:** While a 1-dimensional model captures the primary axis of political conflict (e.g., government vs. opposition or economic left vs. right), legislative voting often involves secondary dimensions (e.g., socially conservative vs. liberal, or European integration).

## 13. Non-Parametric Spatial Scaling: NOMINATE and Optimal Classification (OC)
```

```
## 1. Introduction to Scaling

## 2. Missing Data & Imputation (Pre-processing)
* **The Problem:** Non-parametric matrix methods (like SVD) require a complete, rectangular matrix. Real-world roll-call datasets are inherently incomplete because Members of Parliament (MPs) miss votes due to illness, travel, or strategic abstentions ($NA$).
* **Double-Mean Imputation Formula:**
  $$\\hat{X}_{ij} = \\bar{X}_{i\\cdot} + \\bar{X}_{\\cdot j} - \\bar{X}_{\\cdot\\cdot}$$
  * $\\bar{X}_{i\\cdot}$: The row mean (the average voting tendency of legislator $i$ across all their observed votes).
  * $\\bar{X}_{\\cdot j}$: The column mean (the average support/popularity level of bill $j$ across all observed legislators).
  * $\\bar{X}_{\\cdot\\cdot}$: The grand mean (the overall baseline rate of "yes" votes in the entire dataset).
* **Intuition:** Instead of just filling missing values with a flat global average, double-mean imputation accounts for two facts simultaneously: how agreeable a specific legislator is in general, and how popular that specific bill was across the whole parliament. The grand mean is subtracted to avoid double-counting the baseline dataset density.

---

## 3. Matrix Methods: Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)
* **Mathematical Definition:** Any real rectangular $n \\times m$ matrix $X$ can be factored into three matrices:
  $$X = U D V^T$$
  * $U$ ($n \\times n$ orthogonal matrix): Contains the left-singular vectors. These represent the latent coordinates or positions of the rows (**legislators**).
  * $D$ ($n \\times m$ diagonal matrix): Contains non-negative singular values $d_j$ sorted in descending order ($d_1 \\ge d_2 \\ge \\dots \\ge d_m$). These values scale the importance of each dimension.
  * $V$ ($m \\times m$ orthogonal matrix): Contains the right-singular vectors. These represent the latent coordinates or positions of the columns (**votes/bills**).
* **Low-Rank Approximation (Truncated SVD):** To reduce dimensionality and filter out noise, we retain only the top $k$ dimensions:
  $$X \\approx U_k D_k V_k^T$$
* **Variance Explained:** The proportion of total variance captured by the very first latent dimension is calculated using the singular values from the $D$ component:
  $$var_1 = \\frac{d_1^2}{\\sum_j d_j^2}$$

---

## 4. Advanced Geometric Pre-processing: Double-Centering
* **Motivation:** Raw voting matrices contain strong main effects. For instance, some bills pass almost unanimously, and some legislators vote "yes" to almost everything. If you run SVD on raw data, the first dimension will simply capture these baseline popularities rather than actual ideological differences. Double-centering extracts these main effects before decomposition.
* **Double-Centering Formula:**
  $$\\tilde{X}_{ij} = X_{ij} - \\bar{X}_{i\\cdot} - \\bar{X}_{\\cdot j} + \\bar{X}_{\\cdot\\cdot}$$
* **Mathematical Properties:** The resulting double-centered matrix $\\tilde{X}$ is structurally shifted so that the sum (and mean) of every single row and column equals exactly zero:
  $$\\sum_j \\tilde{X}_{ij} = 0 \\quad \\forall i \\quad \\text{and} \\quad \\sum_i \\tilde{X}_{ij} = 0 \\quad \\forall j$$
* **Intuition:** By stripping away how frequently an MP votes "yes" and how frequently a bill gets "yes" votes, SVD is forced to run strictly on the *interaction* between MPs and bills. This shifts the focus from "who shows up and agrees with the mainstream" to "who deviates from the mainstream in a systematic, polarized way".

## 5. Parametric Models: Item Response Theory (IRT)
* **Concept:** Instead of treating votes as deterministic geometric points, IRT models voting behavior as a probabilistic outcome driven by an underlying latent trait (the ideal point).
* **The 1-Dimensional 2-PL (Two-Parameter Logistic) Model:**
  $$P(Y_{ij} = 1) = \\text{logit}^{-1}(\\beta_j \\theta_i - \\alpha_j) = \\frac{\\exp(\\beta_j \\theta_i - \\alpha_j)}{1 + \\exp(\\beta_j \\theta_i - \\alpha_j)}$$
* **Parameter Breakdown:**

  * $\\beta_j$ (**Discrimination Parameter**): Measures how strongly a vote is driven by ideology. If $\\beta_j$ is high, the bill cleanly splits legislators along ideological lines. If $\\beta_j \\approx 0$, the vote is non-ideological or chaotic.
  * $\\alpha_j$ (**Difficulty / Cutpoint Parameter**): The baseline hurdle or structural propensity to vote "yes" on bill $j$, regardless of ideology.
* **Political Science Equivalence:** This parametric formulation maps perfectly onto the classic spatial voting model (Clinton, Jackman, and Rivers 2004), where political actors maximize utility over policy alternatives under logistic error terms.

## 6. Bayesian Estimation, Priors, and Identification
* **Estimation via MCMC:** Because IRT models feature thousands of parameters simultaneously ($\\theta$, $\\beta$, and $\\alpha$), they are typically estimated using Bayesian Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithms via packages like `brms` (R) or `Stan`/`PyMC` (Python).
* **Standard Informative Priors:**

* **Resolution:** To prevent the scale from flipping arbitrarily during MCMC chains, you must apply post-processing sign constraints—for example, forcing a known left-wing legislator to always have a negative $\\theta$ value, which anchors the direction of the entire spectrum.

* **The SVD vs. IRT Dichotomy:** SVD provides a single, rigid point estimate for each MP, but offers no measure of statistical confidence or error. Bayesian IRT yields a full posterior distribution for every parameter.
* **Two Distinct Levels of Uncertainty:**

## 8. Multidimensional Scaling (MDS) & Proximity Models
* **Core Concept:** Unlike SVD, which directly factorizes the rectangular legislator-by-vote matrix, Multidimensional Scaling (MDS) operates on a square $n \\times n$ **dissimilarity (or distance) matrix** $D$, where each entry $d_{ij}$ represents how "differently" legislator $i$ and legislator $j$ voted across all bills.
* **Classical (Metric) MDS:** Tries to find low-dimensional coordinates $x_i, x_j \\in \\mathbb{R}^k$ such that the Euclidean distances between them match the observed dissimilarities as closely as possible:
  $$\\min_{X} \\sum_{i<j} \\left( \\|x_i - x_j\\| - d_{ij} \\right)^2$$
* **Non-Metric MDS:** Used when voting differences are treated as ordinal rather than interval data. It fits a monotonic transformation $f(d_{ij})$ to preserve only the *rank order* of distances, minimizing an objective function called **Stress**:
  $$\\text{Stress} = \\sqrt{\\frac{\\sum_{i<j} \\left( f(d_{ij}) - \\|x_i - x_j\\| \\right)^2}{\\sum_{i<j} \\|x_i - x_j\\|^2}}$$
* **Intuition:** Imagine you only know the driving distances between a list of cities, but not their geographical coordinates. MDS acts like a mapmaker that uses these distances to reconstruct a 2D map of the cities. In political scaling, "cities" are legislators, and "distance" is how often they disagree.

---

## 9. Dynamic Ideal Point Estimation (Scaling Over Time)
* **The Problem:** In longitudinal datasets (across multiple years or legislative periods like *Wahlperioden*), legislators change their strategic behavior, switch parties, or drift ideologically. Standard static SVD or IRT models assume an MP's position $\\theta_i$ is perfectly constant, which masks temporal polarization.
* **The State-Space / Random Walk Framework:** To allow positions to evolve smoothly over time periods $t$, we specify a dynamic prior for the ideal points:
  $$\\theta_{i,t} \\sim \\mathcal{N}\\left(\\theta_{i,t-1}, \\sigma_\\omega^2\\right)$$
  where $\\sigma_\\omega^2$ is the evolution variance (evolution parameter) controlling how much an MP's position can change from one period to the next.
* **Intuition:** This model balances two opposing forces: the data from period $t$ pulling the MP toward their current voting behavior, and the random walk prior anchoring them to their past self ($\\theta_{i,t-1}$). This prevents erratic, noisy jumps from vote to vote while capturing long-term trends like a party shifting further to the extremes.

---

## 10. Item Characteristic Curves (ICC) & Ideological Cutpoints
* **Visualizing the 2-PL IRT Model:** The probability of an MP voting "yes" ($Y_{ij}=1$) is mapped as an S-shaped logistic curve (the Item Characteristic Curve) along the ideological axis $\\theta$.
* **The Inflection Point (Cutpoint):** The exact location on the ideological spectrum where an MP has an exact 50/50 chance of voting "yes" or "no" is defined by the ratio of the item parameters:
  $$\\theta_{\\text{cut}} = \\frac{\\alpha_j}{\\beta_j}$$
* **Visual Interpretation of Parameters:**
  * **Discrimination ($\\beta_j$) as Slope:** The steeper the curve at the cutpoint, the more heavily polarized the vote is. A perfectly vertical line means everyone to the left votes "no" and everyone to the right votes "yes" (perfect ideological sorting).
  * **Difficulty ($\\alpha_j$) as Shift:** If a bill is highly popular (high baseline agreement), the entire S-curve shifts horizontally, meaning even highly critical or opposition MPs have a high baseline probability of voting "yes".

---

## 11. Model Evaluation, Fit Diagnostics & Baselines
* **The Problem:** How do we know if a 1D or 2D latent space actually explains the parliament, or if MPs are just voting randomly? We need to compare our scaling results against a naive baseline.
* **The Classification Baseline:** The simplest baseline is the **Majority Roll-Call Guess**. If a bill passed with 70% "yes" votes, a naive model simply guesses "yes" for *every* legislator, yielding a baseline accuracy of 70%.
* **Aggregate Proportion of Reduction in Error (APRE):** Measures how many of the baseline classification errors are completely eliminated by introducing our latent ideal points:
  $$\\text{APRE} = \\frac{\\sum_j \\text{Errors}(\\text{Majority}_j) - \\sum_j \\text{Errors}(\\text{Model}_j)}{\\sum_j \\text{Errors}(\\text{Majority}_j)}$$
  * An $\\text{APRE} = 0$ means the spatial model performs no better than just guessing the majority outcome.
  * An $\\text{APRE} = 1$ means the spatial model predicts every single individual vote perfectly.
* **Geometric Mean Probability (GMP):** Evaluates the probabilistic fit of the IRT model by taking the exponent of the average log-likelihood:
  $$\\text{GMP} = \\exp\\left( \\frac{1}{N} \\sum_{i} \\sum_{j} \\ln P(Y_{ij} = y_{ij} \\mid \\theta_i, \\beta_j, \\alpha_j) \\right)$$
  where $N$ is the total number of non-missing votes. GMP ranges from 0.5 (random guessing on binary choices) to 1.0 (absolute certainty).

---

## 12. Multidimensional IRT Models & The Rotation Problem
* **Core Concept:** While a 1-dimensional model captures the primary axis of political conflict (e.g., government vs. opposition or economic left vs. right), legislative voting often involves secondary dimensions (e.g., socially conservative vs. liberal, or European integration).
* **The Multidimensional 2-PL IRT Formula:** For a model with $K$ dimensions, the probability of a "yes" vote expands to a linear combination of traits:
  $$P(Y_{ij} = 1) = \\text{logit}^{-1}\\left( \\sum_{k=1}^K \\beta_{jk} \\theta_{ik} - \\alpha_j \\right)$$
  where $\\theta_{ik}$ is the position of legislator $i$ on dimension $k$, and $\\beta_{jk}$ is the discrimination parameter of bill $j$ on that specific dimension.
* **The Rotational Invariance Problem:** Multidimensional latent spaces suffer from identification issues because the coordinate system can be rotated, inverted, or scaled without altering the underlying likelihood of the observed data:
  $$\\sum_{k=1}^K \\beta_{jk} \\theta_{ik} = \\boldsymbol{\\beta}_j^T \\boldsymbol{\\theta}_i = \\boldsymbol{\\beta}_j^T R^T R \\boldsymbol{\\theta}_i$$
  where $R$ is an orthogonal rotation matrix.
* **Resolution:** To prevent the orientation of the dimensions during estimation, researchers must apply structural constraints, such as forcing specific item discrimination parameters to zero ($\\beta_{j2} = 0$) to anchor the first dimension as the sole predictor for a baseline set of votes.

---

## 13. Non-Parametric Spatial Scaling: NOMINATE and Optimal Classification (OC)
* **Core Concept:** Developed as alternatives to probabilistic IRT, these geometric frameworks view roll-call voting through strict spatial utility theory. Instead of using smooth logistic curves, they find cutting lines or cutting planes that partition the latent space.
* **W-NOMINATE:** Assumes legislators have a bell-shaped (Gaussian) utility function over policy outcomes, combined with a probabilistic error term. It estimates ideal points by maximizing the spatial utility matches across all votes.
* **Optimal Classification (OC):** A purely non-parametric, deterministic alternative. It does not assume any specific probability distribution for errors. Instead, it uses an iterative algorithm to find a line (or hyperplane in multiple dimensions) for each vote that splits the "yes" voters from the "no" voters while strictly minimizing the total number of classification errors.
* **Intuition:** If IRT is like predicting votes based on a shifting probability curve, Optimal Classification is like drawing a hard physical boundary on a map of legislators to separate the "yes" camp from the "no" camp with the absolute minimum number of mistakes.

---

## 14. Extending Scaling Frameworks to Text: Wordfish and Wordscores
* **Core Concept:** The mathematical logic of latent scaling is not restricted to roll-call votes. It can be directly applied to textual data (e.g., party manifestos, parliamentary speeches, or tweets) by treating the usage of specific words exactly like a legislator casting a vote.
* **Wordfish (The Poisson IRT Model):** An unsupervised text-scaling model that models word frequencies $Y_{it}$ (the count of word $w$ used by actor $i$ at time $t$) via a Poisson distribution:
  $$Y_{iwt} \\sim \\text{Poisson}(\\lambda_{iwt})$$
  $$\\ln(\\lambda_{iwt}) = \\alpha_{it} + \\psi_w + \\beta_w \\theta_{it}$$
  * $\\alpha_{it}$: Document fixed effects (captures the overall length/verbosity of speaker $i$ at time $t$).
  * $\\psi_w$: Word fixed effects (captures the baseline frequency of word $w$ across all documents).
  * $\\beta_w$: Word discrimination parameter (how strongly the usage of word $w$ is driven by ideology).
  * $\\theta_{it}$: The latent ideological position of actor $i$ at time $t$.
* **Intuition:** Just as an ideological bill forces a split in a roll-call vote ($\\beta_j$), an ideologically charged word (e.g., "solidarity" vs. "competitiveness") forces a split in word choice. Wordfish tracks these word-selection patterns to map actors on a continuous spectrum without requiring hand-coded text.

---

## 15. Strategic Abstentions & Non-Ignorable Missingness
* **The Hidden Signal:** In standard matrix factorization (SVD), missing votes (`no_show` or `abstain`) are typically treated as missing completely at random ($NA$). However, in parliamentary systems, abstaining or skipping a vote is often a highly strategic choice used by cross-pressured legislators or to signal internal party dissent without breaking party discipline.
* **Methodological Solutions:**
  1. **Ordered IRT Models:** Treat the response as an ordered categorical variable ($0 = \\text{No}$, $1 = \\text{Abstain}$, $2 = \\text{Yes}$) using a cumulative logit link function.
  2. **Joint Selection Models:** Estimate a two-equation system simultaneously—one equation modeling the binary decision to participate in the vote (the selection mechanism), and a second equation modeling the actual choice ($Yes/No$) conditional on participation.
* **Intuition:** When an MP purposefully skips a controversial vote, their absence is a direct reflection of their latent ideological conflict. Modeling this explicitly prevents the ideal points of moderate or rebellious MPs from being biased by simple data imputation.
```

```
# Part 2: Comprehensive Guide to Legislative Scaling and Ideal Point Estimation

## 1. Introduction to Scaling
* **Core Goal:** To place political actors (legislators, parties) and/or choice items (roll-call votes, bills, survey questions) onto a common, low-dimensional latent scale (typically 1-dimensional or 2-dimensional, such as a Left-Right ideological spectrum).
* **Data Inputs:** Primarily binary roll-call voting matrices ($Y_{ij} \\in \\{0, 1\\}$), where rows represent legislators and columns represent votes. It can also be applied to survey responses or text co-occurrence matrices.
* **Methodological Approaches:**
  1. **Matrix Factorization Methods:** Deterministic geometric reductions (e.g., Multidimensional Scaling [MDS], Singular Value Decomposition [SVD], Principal Component Analysis [PCA]).
  2. **Parametric Models:** Probabilistic frameworks based on latent traits (e.g., Item Response Theory [IRT]).
  3. **Non-parametric Models:** Classification techniques optimized for spatial cutting lines (e.g., Optimal Classification).

## 2. Missing Data & Imputation (Pre-processing)
* **The Problem:** Non-parametric matrix methods (like SVD) require a complete, rectangular matrix. Real-world roll-call datasets are inherently incomplete because Members of Parliament (MPs) miss votes due to illness, travel, or strategic abstentions ($NA$).
* **Double-Mean Imputation Formula:**
  $$\\hat{X}_{ij} = \\bar{X}_{i\\cdot} + \\bar{X}_{\\cdot j} - \\bar{X}_{\\cdot\\cdot}$$
  * $\\bar{X}_{i\\cdot}$: The row mean (the average voting tendency of legislator $i$ across all their observed votes).
  * $\\bar{X}_{\\cdot j}$: The column mean (the average support/popularity level of bill $j$ across all observed legislators).
  * $\\bar{X}_{\\cdot\\cdot}$: The grand mean (the overall baseline rate of "yes" votes in the entire dataset).
* **Intuition:** Instead of just filling missing values with a flat global average, double-mean imputation accounts for two facts simultaneously: how agreeable a specific legislator is in general, and how popular that specific bill was across the whole parliament. The grand mean is subtracted to avoid double-counting the baseline dataset density.

## 3. Matrix Methods: Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)
* **Mathematical Definition:** Any real rectangular $n \\times m$ matrix $X$ can be factored into three matrices:
  $$X = U D V^T$$
  * $U$ ($n \\times n$ orthogonal matrix): Contains the left-singular vectors. These represent the latent coordinates or positions of the rows (**legislators**).
  * $D$ ($n \\times m$ diagonal matrix): Contains non-negative singular values $d_j$ sorted in descending order ($d_1 \\ge d_2 \\ge \\dots \\ge d_m$). These values scale the importance of each dimension.
  * $V$ ($m \\times m$ orthogonal matrix): Contains the right-singular vectors. These represent the latent coordinates or positions of the columns (**votes/bills**).
* **Low-Rank Approximation (Truncated SVD):** To reduce dimensionality and filter out noise, we retain only the top $k$ dimensions:
  $$X \\approx U_k D_k V_k^T$$
* **Variance Explained:** The proportion of total variance captured by the very first latent dimension is calculated using the singular values from the $D$ component:
  $$var_1 = \\frac{d_1^2}{\\sum_j d_j^2}$$

## 4. Advanced Geometric Pre-processing: Double-Centering
* **Motivation:** Raw voting matrices contain strong main effects. For instance, some bills pass almost unanimously, and some legislators vote "yes" to almost everything. If you run SVD on raw data, the first dimension will simply capture these baseline popularities rather than actual ideological differences. Double-centering extracts these main effects before decomposition.
* **Double-Centering Formula:**
  $$\\tilde{X}_{ij} = X_{ij} - \\bar{X}_{i\\cdot} - \\bar{X}_{\\cdot j} + \\bar{X}_{\\cdot\\cdot}$$
* **Mathematical Properties:** The resulting double-centered matrix $\\tilde{X}$ is structurally shifted so that the sum (and mean) of every single row and column equals exactly zero:
  $$\\sum_j \\tilde{X}_{ij} = 0 \\quad \\forall i \\quad \\text{and} \\quad \\sum_i \\tilde{X}_{ij} = 0 \\quad \\forall j$$
* **Intuition:** By stripping away how frequently an MP votes "yes" and how frequently a bill gets "yes" votes, SVD is forced to run strictly on the *interaction* between MPs and bills. This shifts the focus from "who shows up and agrees with the mainstream" to "who deviates from the mainstream in a systematic, polarized way".

## 5. Parametric Models: Item Response Theory (IRT)
* **Concept:** Instead of treating votes as deterministic geometric points, IRT models voting behavior as a probabilistic outcome driven by an underlying latent trait (the ideal point).
* **The 1-Dimensional 2-PL (Two-Parameter Logistic) Model:**
  $$P(Y_{ij} = 1) = \\text{logit}^{-1}(\\beta_j \\theta_i - \\alpha_j) = \\frac{\\exp(\\beta_j \\theta_i - \\alpha_j)}{1 + \\exp(\\beta_j \\theta_i - \\alpha_j)}$$
* **Parameter Breakdown:**
  * $\\theta_i$ (**Ideal Point / Latent Trait**): The ideological position of legislator $i$ on the latent scale (e.g., how economically conservative or progressive they are).
  * $\\beta_j$ (**Discrimination Parameter**): Measures how strongly a vote is driven by ideology. If $\\beta_j$ is high, the bill cleanly splits legislators along ideological lines. If $\\beta_j \\approx 0$, the vote is non-ideological or chaotic.
  * $\\alpha_j$ (**Difficulty / Cutpoint Parameter**): The baseline hurdle or structural propensity to vote "yes" on bill $j$, regardless of ideology.
* **Political Science Equivalence:** This parametric formulation maps perfectly onto the classic spatial voting model (Clinton, Jackman, and Rivers 2004), where political actors maximize utility over policy alternatives under logistic error terms.

## 6. Bayesian Estimation, Priors, and Identification
* **Estimation via MCMC:** Because IRT models feature thousands of parameters simultaneously ($\\theta$, $\\beta$, and $\\alpha$), they are typically estimated using Bayesian Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithms via packages like `brms` (R) or `Stan`/`PyMC` (Python).
* **Standard Informative Priors:**
  * $\\theta_i \\sim \\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$: Anchors the location (mean at 0) and scale (variance of 1) of the latent dimension.
  * $beta_j, \\alpha_j \\sim \\text{Weakly informative normal priors}$ (to stabilize estimation).
* **The Identification Problem (Invariance):** The likelihood function of an IRT model cannot distinguish between a specific scale and its mirror image. Multiplying all $\\theta_i$ and $\\beta_j$ by $-1$ results in the exact same probabilities. 
* **Resolution:** To prevent the scale from flipping arbitrarily during MCMC chains, you must apply post-processing sign constraints—for example, forcing a known left-wing legislator to always have a negative $\\theta$ value, which anchors the direction of the entire spectrum.

## 7. Statistical Inference and Uncertainty Analysis
* **The SVD vs. IRT Dichotomy:** SVD provides a single, rigid point estimate for each MP, but offers no measure of statistical confidence or error. Bayesian IRT yields a full posterior distribution for every parameter.
* **Two Distinct Levels of Uncertainty:**
  1. **Individual Position Uncertainty:** Evaluated via the posterior variance or Credible Intervals (e.g., 95% CI) of a single legislator's $\\theta_i$. This indicates how precisely we can pin down that specific individual's ideology based on their voting history.
  2. **Ordinal / Pairwise Uncertainty:** Evaluates whether we can confidently state that Legislator $A$ is to the right of Legislator $B$. Even if their individual 95% CIs overlap slightly, their relative positions might be highly stable across MCMC iterations.
* **Calculating Pairwise Ordering Probability:** The true confidence that legislator $A$ is more conservative than legislator $B$ is extracted by looking across all $S$ samples of the MCMC chains:
  $$P(\\theta_A > \\theta_B \\mid Y) = \\frac{1}{S}\\sum_{s=1}^S \\mathbb{I}(\\theta_{A}^{(s)} > \\theta_{B}^{(s)})$$
  where $\\mathbb{I}$ is an indicator function that equals 1 if the condition is met in sample $s$, and 0 otherwise.

## 8. Multidimensional Scaling (MDS) & Proximity Models
* **Core Concept:** Unlike SVD, which directly factorizes the rectangular legislator-by-vote matrix, Multidimensional Scaling (MDS) operates on a square $n \\times n$ **dissimilarity (or distance) matrix** $D$, where each entry $d_{ij}$ represents how "differently" legislator $i$ and legislator $j$ voted across all bills.
* **Classical (Metric) MDS:** Tries to find low-dimensional coordinates $x_i, x_j \\in \\mathbb{R}^k$ such that the Euclidean distances between them match the observed dissimilarities as closely as possible:
  $$\\min_{X} \\sum_{i<j} \\left( \\|x_i - x_j\\| - d_{ij} \\right)^2$$
* **Non-Metric MDS:** Used when voting differences are treated as ordinal rather than interval data. It fits a monotonic transformation $f(d_{ij})$ to preserve only the *rank order* of distances, minimizing an objective function called **Stress**:
  $$\\text{Stress} = \\sqrt{\\frac{\\sum_{i<j} \\left( f(d_{ij}) - \\|x_i - x_j\\| \\right)^2}{\\sum_{i<j} \\|x_i - x_j\\|^2}}$$
* **Intuition:** Imagine you only know the driving distances between a list of cities, but not their geographical coordinates. MDS acts like a mapmaker that uses these distances to reconstruct a 2D map of the cities. In political scaling, "cities" are legislators, and "distance" is how often they disagree.

## 9. Dynamic Ideal Point Estimation (Scaling Over Time)
* **The Problem:** In longitudinal datasets (across multiple years or legislative periods like *Wahlperioden*), legislators change their strategic behavior, switch parties, or drift ideologically. Standard static SVD or IRT models assume an MP's position $\\theta_i$ is perfectly constant, which masks temporal polarization.
* **The State-Space / Random Walk Framework:** To allow positions to evolve smoothly over time periods $t$, we specify a dynamic prior for the ideal points:
  $$\\theta_{i,t} \\sim \\mathcal{N}\\left(\\theta_{i,t-1}, \\sigma_\\omega^2\\right)$$
  where $\\sigma_\\omega^2$ is the evolution variance (evolution parameter) controlling how much an MP's position can change from one period to the next.
* **Intuition:** This model balances two opposing forces: the data from period $t$ pulling the MP toward their current voting behavior, and the random walk prior anchoring them to their past self ($\\theta_{i,t-1}$). This prevents erratic, noisy jumps from vote to vote while capturing long-term trends like a party shifting further to the extremes.

## 10. Item Characteristic Curves (ICC) & Ideological Cutpoints
* **Visualizing the 2-PL IRT Model:** The probability of an MP voting "yes" ($Y_{ij}=1$) is mapped as an S-shaped logistic curve (the Item Characteristic Curve) along the ideological axis $\\theta$.
* **The Inflection Point (Cutpoint):** The exact location on the ideological spectrum where an MP has an exact 50/50 chance of voting "yes" or "no" is defined by the ratio of the item parameters:
  $$\\theta_{\\text{cut}} = \\frac{\\alpha_j}{\\beta_j}$$
* **Visual Interpretation of Parameters:**
  * **Discrimination ($\\beta_j$) as Slope:** The steeper the curve at the cutpoint, the more heavily polarized the vote is. A perfectly vertical line means everyone to the left votes "no" and everyone to the right votes "yes" (perfect ideological sorting).
  * **Difficulty ($\\alpha_j$) as Shift:** If a bill is highly popular (high baseline agreement), the entire S-curve shifts horizontally, meaning even highly critical or opposition MPs have a high baseline probability of voting "yes".

## 11. Model Evaluation, Fit Diagnostics & Baselines
* **The Problem:** How do we know if a 1D or 2D latent space actually explains the parliament, or if MPs are just voting randomly? We need to compare our scaling results against a naive baseline.
* **The Classification Baseline:** The simplest baseline is the **Majority Roll-Call Guess**. If a bill passed with 70% "yes" votes, a naive model simply guesses "yes" for *every* legislator, yielding a baseline accuracy of 70%.
* **Aggregate Proportion of Reduction in Error (APRE):** Measures how many of the baseline classification errors are completely eliminated by introducing our latent ideal points:
  $$\\text{APRE} = \\frac{\\sum_j \\text{Errors}(\\text{Majority}_j) - \\sum_j \\text{Errors}(\\text{Model}_j)}{\\sum_j \\text{Errors}(\\text{Majority}_j)}$$
  * An $\\text{APRE} = 0$ means the spatial model performs no better than just guessing the majority outcome.
  * An $\\text{APRE} = 1$ means the spatial model predicts every single individual vote perfectly.
* **Geometric Mean Probability (GMP):** Evaluates the probabilistic fit of the IRT model by taking the exponent of the average log-likelihood:
  $$\\text{GMP} = \\exp\\left( \\frac{1}{N} \\sum_{i} \\sum_{j} \\ln P(Y_{ij} = y_{ij} \\mid \\theta_i, \\beta_j, \\alpha_j) \\right)$$
  where $N$ is the total number of non-missing votes. GMP ranges from 0.5 (random guessing on binary choices) to 1.0 (absolute certainty).

## 12. Multidimensional IRT Models & The Rotation Problem
* **Core Concept:** While a 1-dimensional model captures the primary axis of political conflict (e.g., government vs. opposition or economic left vs. right), legislative voting often involves secondary dimensions (e.g., socially conservative vs. liberal, or European integration).
* **The Multidimensional 2-PL IRT Formula:** For a model with $K$ dimensions, the probability of a "yes" vote expands to a linear combination of traits:
  $$P(Y_{ij} = 1) = \\text{logit}^{-1}\\left( \\sum_{k=1}^K \\beta_{jk} \\theta_{ik} - \\alpha_j \\right)$$
  where $\\theta_{ik}$ is the position of legislator $i$ on dimension $k$, and $\\beta_{jk}$ is the discrimination parameter of bill $j$ on that specific dimension.
* **The Rotational Invariance Problem:** Multidimensional latent spaces suffer from identification issues because the coordinate system can be rotated, inverted, or scaled without altering the underlying likelihood of the observed data:
  $$\\sum_{k=1}^K \\beta_{jk} \\theta_{ik} = \\boldsymbol{\\beta}_j^T \\boldsymbol{\\theta}_i = \\boldsymbol{\\beta}_j^T R^T R \\boldsymbol{\\theta}_i$$
  where $R$ is an orthogonal rotation matrix.
* **Resolution:** To prevent the orientation of the dimensions during estimation, researchers must apply structural constraints, such as forcing specific item discrimination parameters to zero ($\\beta_{j2} = 0$) to anchor the first dimension as the sole predictor for a baseline set of votes.

## 13. Non-Parametric Spatial Scaling: NOMINATE and Optimal Classification (OC)
* **Core Concept:** Developed as alternatives to probabilistic IRT, these geometric frameworks view roll-call voting through strict spatial utility theory. Instead of using smooth logistic curves, they find cutting lines or cutting planes that partition the latent space.
* **W-NOMINATE:** Assumes legislators have a bell-shaped (Gaussian) utility function over policy outcomes, combined with a probabilistic error term. It estimates ideal points by maximizing the spatial utility matches across all votes.
* **Optimal Classification (OC):** A purely non-parametric, deterministic alternative. It does not assume any specific probability distribution for errors. Instead, it uses an iterative algorithm to find a line (or hyperplane in multiple dimensions) for each vote that splits the "yes" voters from the "no" voters while strictly minimizing the total number of classification errors.
* **Intuition:** If IRT is like predicting votes based on a shifting probability curve, Optimal Classification is like drawing a hard physical boundary on a map of legislators to separate the "yes" camp from the "no" camp with the absolute minimum number of mistakes.

## 14. Extending Scaling Frameworks to Text: Wordfish and Wordscores
* **Core Concept:** The mathematical logic of latent scaling is not restricted to roll-call votes. It can be directly applied to textual data (e.g., party manifestos, parliamentary speeches, or tweets) by treating the usage of specific words exactly like a legislator casting a vote.
* **Wordfish (The Poisson IRT Model):** An unsupervised text-scaling model that models word frequencies $Y_{it}$ (the count of word $w$ used by actor $i$ at time $t$) via a Poisson distribution:
  $$Y_{iwt} \\sim \\text{Poisson}(\\lambda_{iwt})$$
  $$\\ln(\\lambda_{iwt}) = \\alpha_{it} + \\psi_w + \\beta_w \\theta_{it}$$
  * $\\alpha_{it}$: Document fixed effects (captures the overall length/verbosity of speaker $i$ at time $t$).
  * $\\psi_w$: Word fixed effects (captures the baseline frequency of word $w$ across all documents).
  * $\\beta_w$: Word discrimination parameter (how strongly the usage of word $w$ is driven by ideology).
  * $\\theta_{it}$: The latent ideological position of actor $i$ at time $t$.
* **Intuition:** Just as an ideological bill forces a split in a roll-call vote ($\\beta_j$), an ideologically charged word (e.g., "solidarity" vs. "competitiveness") forces a split in word choice. Wordfish tracks these word-selection patterns to map actors on a continuous spectrum without requiring hand-coded text.

## 15. Strategic Abstentions & Non-Ignorable Missingness
* **The Hidden Signal:** In standard matrix factorization (SVD), missing votes (`no_show` or `abstain`) are typically treated as missing completely at random ($NA$). However, in parliamentary systems, abstaining or skipping a vote is often a highly strategic choice used by cross-pressured legislators or to signal internal party dissent without breaking party discipline.
* **Methodological Solutions:**
  1. **Ordered IRT Models:** Treat the response as an ordered categorical variable ($0 = \\text{No}$, $1 = \\text{Abstain}$, $2 = \\text{Yes}$) using a cumulative logit link function.
  2. **Joint Selection Models:** Estimate a two-equation system simultaneously—one equation modeling the binary decision to participate in the vote (the selection mechanism), and a second equation modeling the actual choice ($Yes/No$) conditional on participation.
* **Intuition:** When an MP purposefully skips a controversial vote, their absence is a direct reflection of their latent ideological conflict. Modeling this explicitly prevents the ideal points of moderate or rebellious MPs from being biased by simple data imputation.
```

```
# Part 2: Comprehensive Guide to Legislative Scaling and Ideal Point Estimation

## 1. Introduction to Scaling
* **Core Goal:** To place political actors (legislators, parties) and/or choice items (roll-call votes, bills, survey questions) onto a common, low-dimensional latent scale (typically 1-dimensional or 2-dimensional, such as a Left-Right ideological spectrum).
* **Data Inputs:** Primarily binary roll-call voting matrices ($Y_{ij} \\in \\{0, 1\\}$), where rows represent legislators and columns represent votes. It can also be applied to survey responses or text co-occurrence matrices.
* **Methodological Approaches:**
  1. **Matrix Factorization Methods:** Deterministic geometric reductions (e.g., Multidimensional Scaling [MDS], Singular Value Decomposition [SVD], Principal Component Analysis [PCA]).
  2. **Parametric Models:** Probabilistic frameworks based on latent traits (e.g., Item Response Theory [IRT]).
  3. **Non-parametric Models:** Classification techniques optimized for spatial cutting lines (e.g., Optimal Classification).

## 2. Missing Data & Imputation (Pre-processing)
* **The Problem:** Non-parametric matrix methods (like SVD) require a complete, rectangular matrix. Real-world roll-call datasets are inherently incomplete because Members of Parliament (MPs) miss votes due to illness, travel, or strategic abstentions ($NA$).
* **Double-Mean Imputation Formula:**
  $$\\hat{X}_{ij} = \\bar{X}_{i\\cdot} + \\bar{X}_{\\cdot j} - \\bar{X}_{\\cdot\\cdot}$$
  * $\\bar{X}_{i\\cdot}$: The row mean (the average voting tendency of legislator $i$ across all their observed votes).
  * $\\bar{X}_{\\cdot j}$: The column mean (the average support/popularity level of bill $j$ across all observed legislators).
  * $\\bar{X}_{\\cdot\\cdot}$: The grand mean (the overall baseline rate of "yes" votes in the entire dataset).
* **Intuition:** Instead of just filling missing values with a flat global average, double-mean imputation accounts for two facts simultaneously: how agreeable a specific legislator is in general, and how popular that specific bill was across the whole parliament. The grand mean is subtracted to avoid double-counting the baseline dataset density.

## 3. Matrix Methods: Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)
* **Mathematical Definition:** Any real rectangular $n \\times m$ matrix $X$ can be factored into three matrices:
  $$X = U D V^T$$
  * $U$ ($n \\times n$ orthogonal matrix): Contains the left-singular vectors. These represent the latent coordinates or positions of the rows (**legislators**).
  * $D$ ($n \\times m$ diagonal matrix): Contains non-negative singular values $d_j$ sorted in descending order ($d_1 \\ge d_2 \\ge \\dots \\ge d_m$). These values scale the importance of each dimension.
  * $V$ ($m \\times m$ orthogonal matrix): Contains the right-singular vectors. These represent the latent coordinates or positions of the columns (**votes/bills**).
* **Low-Rank Approximation (Truncated SVD):** To reduce dimensionality and filter out noise, we retain only the top $k$ dimensions:
  $$X \\approx U_k D_k V_k^T$$
* **Variance Explained:** The proportion of total variance captured by the very first latent dimension is calculated using the singular values from the $D$ component:
  $$var_1 = \\frac{d_1^2}{\\sum_j d_j^2}$$

## 4. Advanced Geometric Pre-processing: Double-Centering
* **Motivation:** Raw voting matrices contain strong main effects. For instance, some bills pass almost unanimously, and some legislators vote "yes" to almost everything. If you run SVD on raw data, the first dimension will simply capture these baseline popularities rather than actual ideological differences. Double-centering extracts these main effects before decomposition.
* **Double-Centering Formula:**
  $$\\tilde{X}_{ij} = X_{ij} - \\bar{X}_{i\\cdot} - \\bar{X}_{\\cdot j} + \\bar{X}_{\\cdot\\cdot}$$
* **Mathematical Properties:** The resulting double-centered matrix $\\tilde{X}$ is structurally shifted so that the sum (and mean) of every single row and column equals exactly zero:
  $$\\sum_j \\tilde{X}_{ij} = 0 \\quad \\forall i \\quad \\text{and} \\quad \\sum_i \\tilde{X}_{ij} = 0 \\quad \\forall j$$
* **Intuition:** By stripping away how frequently an MP votes "yes" and how frequently a bill gets "yes" votes, SVD is forced to run strictly on the *interaction* between MPs and bills. This shifts the focus from "who shows up and agrees with the mainstream" to "who deviates from the mainstream in a systematic, polarized way".

## 5. Parametric Models: Item Response Theory (IRT)
* **Concept:** Instead of treating votes as deterministic geometric points, IRT models voting behavior as a probabilistic outcome driven by an underlying latent trait (the ideal point).
* **The 1-Dimensional 2-PL (Two-Parameter Logistic) Model:**
  $$P(Y_{ij} = 1) = \\text{logit}^{-1}(\\beta_j \\theta_i - \\alpha_j) = \\frac{\\exp(\\beta_j \\theta_i - \\alpha_j)}{1 + \\exp(\\beta_j \\theta_i - \\alpha_j)}$$
* **Parameter Breakdown:**
  * $\\theta_i$ (**Ideal Point / Latent Trait**): The ideological position of legislator $i$ on the latent scale (e.g., how economically conservative or progressive they are).
  * $\\beta_j$ (**Discrimination Parameter**): Measures how strongly a vote is driven by ideology. If $\\beta_j$ is high, the bill cleanly splits legislators along ideological lines. If $\\beta_j \\approx 0$, the vote is non-ideological or chaotic.
  * $\\alpha_j$ (**Difficulty / Cutpoint Parameter**): The baseline hurdle or structural propensity to vote "yes" on bill $j$, regardless of ideology.
* **Political Science Equivalence:** This parametric formulation maps perfectly onto the classic spatial voting model (Clinton, Jackman, and Rivers 2004), where political actors maximize utility over policy alternatives under logistic error terms.

## 6. Bayesian Estimation, Priors, and Identification
* **Estimation via MCMC:** Because IRT models feature thousands of parameters simultaneously ($\\theta$, $\\beta$, and $\\alpha$), they are typically estimated using Bayesian Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithms via packages like `brms` (R) or `Stan`/`PyMC` (Python).
* **Standard Informative Priors:**
  * $\\theta_i \\sim \\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$: Anchors the location (mean at 0) and scale (variance of 1) of the latent dimension.
  * $\\beta_j, \\alpha_j \\sim \\text{Weakly informative normal priors}$ (to stabilize estimation).
* **The Identification Problem (Invariance):** The likelihood function of an IRT model cannot distinguish between a specific scale and its mirror image. Multiplying all $\\theta_i$ and $\\beta_j$ by $-1$ results in the exact same probabilities. 
* **Resolution:** To prevent the scale from flipping arbitrarily during MCMC chains, you must apply post-processing sign constraints—for example, forcing a known left-wing legislator to always have a negative $\\theta$ value, which anchors the direction of the entire spectrum.

## 7. Statistical Inference and Uncertainty Analysis
* **The SVD vs. IRT Dichotomy:** SVD provides a single, rigid point estimate for each MP, but offers no measure of statistical confidence or error. Bayesian IRT yields a full posterior distribution for every parameter.
* **Two Distinct Levels of Uncertainty:**
  1. **Individual Position Uncertainty:** Evaluated via the posterior variance or Credible Intervals (e.g., 95% CI) of a single legislator's $\\theta_i$. This indicates how precisely we can pin down that specific individual's ideology based on their voting history.
  2. **Ordinal / Pairwise Uncertainty:** Evaluates whether we can confidently state that Legislator $A$ is to the right of Legislator $B$. Even if their individual 95% CIs overlap slightly, their relative positions might be highly stable across MCMC iterations.
* **Calculating Pairwise Ordering Probability:** The true confidence that legislator $A$ is more conservative than legislator $B$ is extracted by looking across all $S$ samples of the MCMC chains:
  $$P(\\theta_A > \\theta_B \\mid Y) = \\frac{1}{S}\\sum_{s=1}^S \\mathbb{I}(\\theta_{A}^{(s)} > \\theta_{B}^{(s)})$$
  where $\\mathbb{I}$ is an indicator function that equals 1 if the condition is met in sample $s$, and 0 otherwise.

## 8. Multidimensional Scaling (MDS) & Proximity Models
* **Core Concept:** Unlike SVD, which directly factorizes the rectangular legislator-by-vote matrix, Multidimensional Scaling (MDS) operates on a square $n \\times n$ **dissimilarity (or distance) matrix** $D$, where each entry $d_{ij}$ represents how "differently" legislator $i$ and legislator $j$ voted across all bills.
* **Classical (Metric) MDS:** Tries to find low-dimensional coordinates $x_i, x_j \\in \\mathbb{R}^k$ such that the Euclidean distances between them match the observed dissimilarities as closely as possible:
  $$\\min_{X} \\sum_{i<j} \\left( \\|x_i - x_j\\| - d_{ij} \\right)^2$$
* **Non-Metric MDS:** Used when voting differences are treated as ordinal rather than interval data. It fits a monotonic transformation $f(d_{ij})$ to preserve only the *rank order* of distances, minimizing an objective function called **Stress**:
  $$\\text{Stress} = \\sqrt{\\frac{\\sum_{i<j} \\left( f(d_{ij} - \\|x_i - x_j\\| \\right)^2}{\\sum_{i<j} \\|x_i - x_j\\|^2}}$$
* **Intuition:** Imagine you only know the driving distances between a list of cities, but not their geographical coordinates. MDS acts like a mapmaker that uses these distances to reconstruct a 2D map of the cities. In political scaling, "cities" are legislators, and "distance" is how often they disagree.

## 9. Dynamic Ideal Point Estimation (Scaling Over Time)
* **The Problem:** In longitudinal datasets (across multiple years or legislative periods like *Wahlperioden*), legislators change their strategic behavior, switch parties, or drift ideologically. Standard static SVD or IRT models assume an MP's position $\\theta_i$ is perfectly constant, which masks temporal polarization.
* **The State-Space / Random Walk Framework:** To allow positions to evolve smoothly over time periods $t$, we specify a dynamic prior for the ideal points:
  $$\\theta_{i,t} \\sim \\mathcal{N}\\left(\\theta_{i,t-1}, \\sigma_\\omega^2\\right)$$
  where $\\sigma_\\omega^2$ is the evolution variance (evolution parameter) controlling how much an MP's position can change from one period to the next.
* **Intuition:** This model balances two opposing forces: the data from period $t$ pulling the MP toward their current voting behavior, and the random walk prior anchoring them to their past self ($\\theta_{i,t-1}$). This prevents erratic, noisy jumps from vote to vote while capturing long-term trends like a party shifting further to the extremes.

## 10. Item Characteristic Curves (ICC) & Ideological Cutpoints
* **Visualizing the 2-PL IRT Model:** The probability of an MP voting "yes" ($Y_{ij}=1$) is mapped as an S-shaped logistic curve (the Item Characteristic Curve) along the ideological axis $\\theta$.
* **The Inflection Point (Cutpoint):** The exact location on the ideological spectrum where an MP has an exact 50/50 chance of voting "yes" or "no" is defined by the ratio of the item parameters:
  $$\\theta_{\\text{cut}} = \\frac{\\alpha_j}{\\beta_j}$$
* **Visual Interpretation of Parameters:**
  * **Discrimination ($\\beta_j$) as Slope:** The steeper the curve at the cutpoint, the more heavily polarized the vote is. A perfectly vertical line means everyone to the left votes "no" and everyone to the right votes "yes" (perfect ideological sorting).
  * **Difficulty ($\\alpha_j$) as Shift:** If a bill is highly popular (high baseline agreement), the entire S-curve shifts horizontally, meaning even highly critical or opposition MPs have a high baseline probability of voting "yes".

## 11. Model Evaluation, Fit Diagnostics & Baselines
* **The Problem:** How do we know if a 1D or 2D latent space actually explains the parliament, or if MPs are just voting randomly? We need to compare our scaling results against a naive baseline.
* **The Classification Baseline:** The simplest baseline is the **Majority Roll-Call Guess**. If a bill passed with 70% "yes" votes, a naive model simply guesses "yes" for *every* legislator, yielding a baseline accuracy of 70%.
* **Aggregate Proportion of Reduction in Error (APRE):** Measures how many of the baseline classification errors are completely eliminated by introducing our latent ideal points:
  $$\\text{APRE} = \\frac{\\sum_j \\text{Errors}(\\text{Majority}_j) - \\sum_j \\text{Errors}(\\text{Model}_j)}{\\sum_j \\text{Errors}(\\text{Majority}_j)}$$
  * An $\\text{APRE} = 0$ means the spatial model performs no better than just guessing the majority outcome.
  * An $\\text{APRE} = 1$ means the spatial model predicts every single individual vote perfectly.
* **Geometric Mean Probability (GMP):** Evaluates the probabilistic fit of the IRT model by taking the exponent of the average log-likelihood:
  $$\\text{GMP} = \\exp\\left( \\frac{1}{N} \\sum_{i} \\sum_{j} \\ln P(Y_{ij} = y_{ij} \\mid \\theta_i, \\beta_j, \\alpha_j) \\right)$$
  where $N$ is the total number of non-missing votes. GMP ranges from 0.5 (random guessing on binary choices) to 1.0 (absolute certainty).

## 12. Multidimensional IRT Models & The Rotation Problem
* **Core Concept:** While a 1-dimensional model captures the primary axis of political conflict (e.g., government vs. opposition or economic left vs. right), legislative voting often involves secondary dimensions (e.g., socially conservative vs. liberal, or European integration).
* **The Multidimensional 2-PL IRT Formula:** For a model with $K$ dimensions, the probability of a "yes" vote expands to a linear combination of traits:
  $$P(Y_{ij} = 1) = \\text{logit}^{-1}\\left( \\sum_{k=1}^K \\beta_{jk} \\theta_{ik} - \\alpha_j \\right)$$
  where $\\theta_{ik}$ is the position of legislator $i$ on dimension $k$, and $\\beta_{jk}$ is the discrimination parameter of bill $j$ on that specific dimension.
* **The Rotational Invariance Problem:** Multidimensional latent spaces suffer from identification issues because the coordinate system can be rotated, inverted, or scaled without altering the underlying likelihood of the observed data:
  $$\\sum_{k=1}^K \\beta_{jk} \\theta_{ik} = \\boldsymbol{\\beta}_j^T \\boldsymbol{\\theta}_i = \\boldsymbol{\\beta}_j^T R^T R \\boldsymbol{\\theta}_i$$
  where $R$ is an orthogonal rotation matrix.
* **Resolution:** To prevent the orientation of the dimensions during estimation, researchers must apply structural constraints, such as forcing specific item discrimination parameters to zero ($\\beta_{j2} = 0$) to anchor the first dimension as the sole predictor for a baseline set of votes.

## 13. Non-Parametric Spatial Scaling: NOMINATE and Optimal Classification (OC)
* **Core Concept:** Developed as alternatives to probabilistic IRT, these geometric frameworks view roll-call voting through strict spatial utility theory. Instead of using smooth logistic curves, they find cutting lines or cutting planes that partition the latent space.
* **W-NOMINATE:** Assumes legislators have a bell-shaped (Gaussian) utility function over policy outcomes, combined with a probabilistic error term. It estimates ideal points by maximizing the spatial utility matches across all votes.
* **Optimal Classification (OC):** A purely non-parametric, deterministic alternative. It does not assume any specific probability distribution for errors. Instead, it uses an iterative algorithm to find a line (or hyperplane in multiple dimensions) for each vote that splits the "yes" voters from the "no" voters while strictly minimizing the total number of classification errors.
* **Intuition:** If IRT is like predicting votes based on a shifting probability curve, Optimal Classification is like drawing a hard physical boundary on a map of legislators to separate the "yes" camp from the "no" camp with the absolute minimum number of mistakes.

## 14. Extending Scaling Frameworks to Text: Wordfish and Wordscores
* **Core Concept:** The mathematical logic of latent scaling is not restricted to roll-call votes. It can be directly applied to textual data (e.g., party manifestos, parliamentary speeches, or tweets) by treating the usage of specific words exactly like a legislator casting a vote.
* **Wordfish (The Poisson IRT Model):** An unsupervised text-scaling model that models word frequencies $Y_{it}$ (the count of word $w$ used by actor $i$ at time $t$) via a Poisson distribution:
  $$Y_{iwt} \\sim \\text{Poisson}(\\lambda_{iwt})$$
  $$\\ln(\\lambda_{iwt}) = \\alpha_{it} + \\psi_w + \\beta_w \\theta_{it}$$
  * $\\alpha_{it}$: Document fixed effects (captures the overall length/verbosity of speaker $i$ at time $t$).
  * $\\psi_w$: Word fixed effects (captures the baseline frequency of word $w$ across all documents).
  * $\\beta_w$: Word discrimination parameter (how strongly the usage of word $w$ is driven by ideology).
  * $\\theta_{it}$: The latent ideological position of actor $i$ at time $t$.
* **Intuition:** Just as an ideological bill forces a split in a roll-call vote ($\\beta_j$), an ideologically charged word (e.g., "solidarity" vs. "competitiveness") forces a split in word choice. Wordfish tracks these word-selection patterns to map actors on a continuous spectrum without requiring hand-coded text.

## 15. Strategic Abstentions & Non-Ignorable Missingness
* **The Hidden Signal:** In standard matrix factorization (SVD), missing votes (`no_show` or `abstain`) are typically treated as missing completely at random ($NA$). However, in parliamentary systems, abstaining or skipping a vote is often a highly strategic choice used by cross-pressured legislators or to signal internal party dissent without breaking party discipline.
* **Methodological Solutions:**
  1. **Ordered IRT Models:** Treat the response as an ordered categorical variable ($0 = \\text{No}$, $1 = \\text{Abstain}$, $2 = \\text{Yes}$) using a cumulative logit link function.
  2. **Joint Selection Models:** Estimate a two-equation system simultaneously—one equation modeling the binary decision to participate in the vote (the selection mechanism), and a second equation modeling the actual choice ($Yes/No$) conditional on participation.
* **Intuition:** When an MP purposefully skips a controversial vote, their absence is a direct reflection of their latent ideological conflict. Modeling this explicitly prevents the ideal points of moderate or rebellious MPs from being biased by simple data imputation.
```

18\. Solving the Identification Problem via Priors
--------------------------------------------------

*   **The Problem:** As discussed in Chapter 6, IRT models suffer from scaling invariance. Without constraints, the MCMC sampler will wander infinitely, causing severe non-convergence.
*   **Bürkner's Solution (Variance Anchoring):** Instead of fixing individual parameters to arbitrary values, Bürkner weakly identifies the model by fixing the standard deviation of the latent person-parameter exactly to 1.
*   **The Code Implementation:**
    ```

      # 1. Anchor the latent scale variance to 1 (CRITICAL)
      prior("constant(1)", class = "sd", group = "mandate_id", nlpar = "theta") +
      # 2. Weakly informative priors for bill parameters
      prior("normal(0, 3)", class = "sd", group = "poll_id", nlpar = "theta") +
    ```
